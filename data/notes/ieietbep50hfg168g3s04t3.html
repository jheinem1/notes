Terminology

The general first-order linear differential equation has the form

dydt+p(t)y=q(t),\frac{dy}{dt}+p(t)y=q(t)\text,dtdy​+p(t)y=q(t),

where ppp and qqq are given functions of ttt. This equation is linear because yyy and y′y'y′ appear to the first power and not in products or compositions involving yyy or y′y'y′.

Order

The order of a differential equation is the highest order that appears on a derivative in the equation.

Of the three examples, the first two are first order, and the third is second order.

Linear

A differential equation is linear if the unknown function yyy and its derivatives appear only to the first power and are not composed with other functions. A linear equation also cannot have products or quotients of yyy and its derivatives.

Of the examples, the first two are linear, and the third is nonlinear (because the right side contains y2y^2y2).

A linear differential equation cannot have terms such as y2y^2y2, yy′yy'yy′, or sin⁡y\sin{y}siny, where yyy is the unknown function.

General Solution

Differential equations can be solved through integration. You must "undo" the derivative y′(t)y'(t)y′(t) in order to find yyy.

The most general solution of a first-order differential equation involves one arbitrary constant, whereas a second-order differential equation requires two arbitrary constants. This carries onto equations of nnnth order, where they have nnn arbitrary constants.

Initial Conditions

A differential equation is often accompanied by initial conditions that specify the values of yyy and possibly y′y'y′ at the start of the problem.

In general, a differential equation of the nnnth order requires nnn initial conditions, which are used to determine the nnn arbitrary constants in the general solution.

A differential equation with the appropriate initial conditions is called an initial value problem. A first-order initial value problem generally has the form
